The Crown Jewel of India

The Taj Mahal is an ivory-white marble mausoleum located on the southern bank of the Yamuna river in the Indian city of Agra. It was commissioned in 1632 by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan to house the tomb of his favorite wife, Mumtaz Mahal. The Taj Mahal is recognized as the jewel of Muslim art in India and one of the universally admired masterpieces of the world's heritage.

Built over 20 years by 20,000 artisans under the guidance of architect Ustad Ahmad Lahauri, the Taj Mahal combines elements from Islamic, Persian, Ottoman Turkish and Indian architectural styles. The complex covers 42 acres and includes a mosque, guest house, and formal gardens.

Architectural Marvel

The Taj Mahal represents the finest and most sophisticated example of Indo-Islamic architecture. Its origins lie in the moving circumstances of its commission and the culture and history of an Islamic Mughal empire ruling in India.

The symmetrical complex is set around formal gardens and raises on a square plinth. The marble dome that surmounts the tomb is its most spectacular feature. Its height is about 35 meters (115 ft) and is accentuated as it sits on a cylindrical "drum" about 7 meters (23 ft) high.

The minarets, each more than 40 meters (130 ft) tall, display the designer's penchant for symmetry. They were designed as working minarets — a traditional element of mosques, used by the muezzin to call the Islamic faithful to prayer.

Historical Significance

The Taj Mahal was built by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his third wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who died giving birth to their 14th child in 1631. The emperor was devastated by her death and envisioned a memorial that would befit her beauty and their love.

Construction began in 1632 and took approximately 22 years to complete, employing about 20,000 workers from India, Persia, the Ottoman Empire, and Europe. The total cost at the time has been estimated to be about 32 million rupees, which in modern currency would be over $1 billion.

The Taj Mahal is considered the finest example of Mughal architecture, blending Persian, Islamic and Indian styles. In 1983, it became a UNESCO World Heritage Site and has been described as "a teardrop on the cheek of eternity" by Indian poet Rabindranath Tagore.
